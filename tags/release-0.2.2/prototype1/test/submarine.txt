<p>A <b>submarine</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Watercraft" title="Watercraft">watercraft</a> that can operate underwater. Submarines were first widely used in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and are used by all major <a href="/wiki/Navy" title="Navy">navies</a> today. Civilian submarines and <a href="/wiki/Submersibles" title="Submersibles">submersibles</a> are used for <a href="/wiki/Marine_science" title="Marine science">marine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> science and for work at depths too great for human divers. Submarines are typically referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">boats</a>" even though most modern submarines should technically be called "<a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ships</a>". The term <a href="/wiki/U-Boat" title="U-Boat">U-Boat</a> is sometimes used for German submarines in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>. This comes from the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> word for submarine, 'U-Boot', itself an <a href="/wiki/Abbreviation" title="Abbreviation">abbreviation</a> for <i>Unterseeboot</i> ('undersea boat'). The vertical structure, usually located amidships, houses communications and sensing devices as well as periscopes. In modern submarines it is the "sail"; a submarine "<a href="/wiki/Conning_tower" title="Conning tower">conning tower</a>" was a feature of earlier designs: a separate pressure hull above the main body of the boat that allowed the use of shorter periscopes.</p>
<p>Submarines encompass one of the largest ranges in capabilities of any vessel. They range from small one- or two-man vessels that can examine the <a href="/wiki/Sea_floor" title="Sea floor">sea floor</a> for a few hours to the <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_class_submarine" title="Typhoon class submarine">Typhoon class</a>, which can remain submerged for 6 months and carry nuclear missiles capable of destroying multiple <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">cities</a>. There are also specialized submarines such as rescue submarines (like the <a href="/wiki/Deep_Submergence_Rescue_Vehicle" title="Deep Submergence Rescue Vehicle">DSRV</a> or <a href="/wiki/Priz_class" title="Priz class"><i>Priz</i></a>) and tiny one-person human powered subs intended for competitions between <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a>. An older device for use in underwater exploration, salvage, construction and rescue is the <a href="/wiki/Diving_bell" title="Diving bell">diving bell</a>. A specialized form of submarine capable of extremely deep dives is the <a href="/wiki/Bathyscaphe" title="Bathyscaphe">bathyscaphe</a>.</p>
<p>The word <b>submarine</b> was originally an adjective meaning "under the sea". Some firms who make <a href="/wiki/Diving" title="Diving">diving gear</a> but not parts for submarines, called their work "submarine engineering". "Submarine" as a noun meaning a submersible craft originated as short for "submarine boat" and older books such as <i><a href="/wiki/Twenty_Thousand_Leagues_Under_the_Sea" title="Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea">Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea</a></i> always use this term. Also, some people simply say 'sub' instead of saying the entire word 'submarine'.</p>

<p>The roles of military submarines are almost limitless. From delivering supplies and facilitating troop movements undetected, to launching a surprise nuclear attack from just a few miles off of the coast. Submarines have been used as spy platforms and as underwater research vessels.</p>
<p>Submarines are useful militarily because of their ability to remain concealed: they are difficult to find and destroy when deep below the surface. Because <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>'s dense molecular structure makes it an excellent conductor of <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a>, a submarine is more likely to be detected from its sound patterns than its visual appearance. To maintain stealth, a great deal of attention in the design of a submarine is devoted to making it travel through the water as silently as possible. Some submarines conceal their sound so well that they actually create a silent area in their environment, which can be detected if it blocks other ambient sounds. If a submarine remains undetected, it is able to strike at close range.</p>
<p>A concealed submarine can force an enemy Navy to waste resources defending large areas of ocean against possible attack, while in reality only threatening a small area. This advantage was vividly demonstrated in the 1982 <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a> when the British SSN <a href="/wiki/HMS_Conqueror_%28S48%29" title="HMS Conqueror (S48)">HMS <i>Conqueror</i></a> sank the Argentine cruiser <i><a href="/wiki/General_Belgrano" title="General Belgrano">General Belgrano</a></i>. After the sinking the Argentine Navy realised that they were vulnerable to submarine attack, and that they had no defense from it. Thus the Argentinian fleet remained in port for the remainder of the war.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Technology</span></h2>
<p><a name="Submersion_and_navigation" id="Submersion_and_navigation"></a></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Submersion and navigation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Submersion and navigation</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:Submarine_control_surfaces2.svg" class="internal" title="Control surfaces"><img alt="Control surfaces" longdesc="/wiki/Image:Submarine_control_surfaces2.svg" class="thumbimage" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Submarine_control_surfaces2.svg/300px-Submarine_control_surfaces2.svg.png" width="300" height="99" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/wiki/Image:Submarine_control_surfaces2.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Control surfaces</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>All surface ships, as well as surfaced submarines, are in a positively <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyant</a> condition, weighing less than the volume of water they displace. To submerge hydrostatically, a ship must gain negative buoyancy, either increasing its own weight or decreasing displacement of the water. To control their weight, submarines are equipped with ballast tanks, which can be filled with either outside water or pressurized air.</p>
<p>For general submersion or surfacing, submarines use the forward and aft tanks, called Main Ballast Tanks or MBTs, which are opened and completely filled with water to submerge, or filled by pressurized air to surface. Under submerged conditions, MBTs generally always stay flooded, which simplifies their design, so on many submarines these tanks are simply a section of interhull space. For more precise and quick control of depth, submarines use smaller Depth Control Tanks or DCTs, also called hard tanks due to their ability to withstand higher pressure. The amount of water in depth control tanks can be controlled either to reflect changes in outside conditions or change submersion depth. Depth control tanks can be located either near the submarine's <a href="/wiki/Center_of_gravity" title="Center of gravity">center of gravity</a>, or separated along the submarine body to prevent affecting <a href="/wiki/Trim" title="Trim">trim</a>.</p>
<p>When submerged, the water pressure on submarine's hull can reach 4 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_%28unit%29" title="Pascal (unit)">MPa</a> for steel submarines and up to 10 MPa for titanium submarines like <a href="/wiki/Soviet_submarine_K-278" title="Soviet submarine K-278">Komsomolets</a>, while the pressure inside stays the same. This difference results in hull compression, which decreases displacement. Water density also increases, as the <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> and pressure are higher, but this does not compensate for hull compression, so buoyancy falls with depth. A submerged submarine is in an unstable equilibrium, having a tendency to either fall down to the ocean floor or float up to the surface. Keeping a constant depth requires continual operation of either the depth control tanks or control surfaces.<sup id="_ref-Physics_Of_Liquids_.26_Gases_0" class="reference"><a href="#_note-Physics_Of_Liquids_.26_Gases" title="">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Submarines in a neutral buoyancy condition are not intrinsically stable in trim. To sustain desired trim, submarines use specialized forward and aft trim tanks. Pumps can move water between these tanks, changing the weight distribution and therefore creating a moment to turn the sub upwards or downwards. A similar system is sometimes used to maintain stability.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:Kiosk_Casabianca.jpg" class="internal" title="Sail of the French nuclear submarine Casabianca; note the diving planes, camouflaged masts, periscope, electronic warfare masts, door and windows."><img alt="Sail of the French nuclear submarine Casabianca; note the diving planes, camouflaged masts, periscope, electronic warfare masts, door and windows." longdesc="/wiki/Image:Kiosk_Casabianca.jpg" class="thumbimage" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Kiosk_Casabianca.jpg/250px-Kiosk_Casabianca.jpg" width="250" height="161" /></a>

</div>
<p>The hydrostatic effect of variable ballast tanks is not the only way to control the submarine underwater. Hydrodynamic maneuvering is done by several surfaces, which can be turned to create corresponding hydrodynamic forces when a submarine moves at sufficient speed. The stern planes, located near the propeller and normally oriented horizontally, serve the same purpose as the trim tanks, controlling the trim, and are commonly used, while other control surfaces may not be present on many submarines. The fairwater planes on the sail and/or bow planes on the main body, both also horizontal, are located closer to the centre of gravity, and are used to control depth with less effect on the trim.</p>
<p>When a submarine performs an emergency surfacing, all depth and trim methods are used simultaneously, together with propelling the boat upwards. Such surfacing is very quick, so the sub may even partially jump out of the water, but it inflicts serious damage on some submarine systems, primarily pipes.</p>
<p>Modern submarines use an <a href="/wiki/Inertial_guidance_system" title="Inertial guidance system">inertial guidance system</a> for navigation while submerged, but drift error unavoidably builds up over time. To counter this, the <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">Global Positioning System</a> will occasionally be used to obtain an accurate position. The <a href="/wiki/Periscope" title="Periscope">periscope</a> - a retractable <a href="/wiki/Tube" title="Tube">tube</a> with <a href="/wiki/Prism_%28optics%29" title="Prism (optics)">prisms</a> allowing a view to the surface - is only used occasionally in modern submarines, since the range of visibility is short. The <a href="/wiki/Virginia_class_submarine" title="Virginia class submarine"><i>Virginia</i>-class submarines</a> have "photonics masts" rather than hull-penetrating optical tube periscopes. These masts must still be hoisted above the surface, and employ electronic sensors for visible light, infrared, laser range-finding, and electromagnetic surveillance.</p>
<p><a name="Ship_hull" id="Ship_hull"></a></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ship hull">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Ship hull</span></h3>
<p><a name="Overview" id="Overview"></a></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Overview</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:USS_Greeneville_%28SSN_772%29_in_Dry_Dock.jpg" class="internal" title="The Los Angeles class attack submarine USS Greeneville in dry dock, showing typical cigar-shaped hull."><img alt="The Los Angeles class attack submarine USS Greeneville in dry dock, showing typical cigar-shaped hull." longdesc="/wiki/Image:USS_Greeneville_%28SSN_772%29_in_Dry_Dock.jpg" class="thumbimage" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/19/USS_Greeneville_%28SSN_772%29_in_Dry_Dock.jpg/180px-USS_Greeneville_%28SSN_772%29_in_Dry_Dock.jpg" width="180" height="135" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/wiki/Image:USS_Greeneville_%28SSN_772%29_in_Dry_Dock.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The <i>Los Angeles</i> class attack submarine <a href="/wiki/USS_Greeneville_%28SSN-772%29" title="USS Greeneville (SSN-772)">USS <i>Greeneville</i></a> in dry dock, showing typical cigar-shaped hull.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Modern submarines are usually cigar-shaped. This design, already visible on very early submarines (see below) is sometimes called a "teardrop hull". It significantly reduces the hydrodynamic <a href="/wiki/Drag_%28physics%29" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a> on the sub when submerged, but decreases the sea-keeping capabilities and increases the drag while surfaced. Since the limitations of the propulsion systems of early military submarines forced them to operate on the surface most of the time, their hull designs were a compromise. Because of the slow submerged speeds of those subs, usually well below 10<a href="/wiki/Knot_%28speed%29" title="Knot (speed)">kt</a> (18&#160;km·h<sup>−1</sup>), the increased drag for underwater travel was considered acceptable. Only late in World War II, when technology allowed faster and longer submerged operations and increased surveillance by enemy aircraft forced submarines to stay submerged, did hull designs become teardrop shaped again, to reduce drag and noise. On modern military submarines the outer hull is covered with a thick layer of special sound-absorbing <a href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">rubber</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Anechoic_tile" title="Anechoic tile">anechoic plating</a>, to make the submarine quieter.</p>
<p>A raised tower on top of a submarine accommodates the length of the <a href="/wiki/Periscope" title="Periscope">periscope</a> and electronics masts, which can include radio, <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_warfare" title="Electronic warfare">electronic warfare</a>, and other systems. In many early classes of submarines (see history), the Control Room, or "Conn", was located inside this tower, which was known as the "<a href="/wiki/Conning_tower" title="Conning tower">conning tower</a>". Since that time, however, the Conn has been located within the hull of the submarine, and the tower is more commonly called the "sail" today. The Conn should not be confused with the "bridge", which is a small, open platform set into the top of the sail used for visual observation while operating on the surface. There may also be an additional closed platform below this with windows and wipers for bad weather.</p>
<p><a name="Double_hull" id="Double_hull"></a></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Double hull">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Double hull</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Modern submarines and submersibles, as well as the oldest ones, have a single hull. Large submarines generally have an additional hull or hull sections outside. This external hull, which actually forms the shape of submarine, is called the outer hull or <a href="/wiki/Light_hull" title="Light hull">light hull</a>, as it does not have to hold any pressure difference. Inside the outer hull there is a strong hull, or <a href="/wiki/Pressure_hull" title="Pressure hull">pressure hull</a>, which withstands sea pressure and has normal atmospheric pressure inside.</p>
<p>As early as World War I, it was realized that the optimal shape for withstanding pressure conflicted with the optimal shape for seaworthiness and minimized water resistance, and construction difficulties further complicated the problem. This was solved either by a compromise shape, or by using two hulls; internal for holding pressure, and external for optimal shape. Until the end of World War II, most submarines had an additional partial cover on the top, bow and stern, built of thinner metal, which was flooded when submerged. Germany went further with the <a href="/wiki/Type_XXI" title="Type XXI">Type XXI</a>, the general predecessor of modern submarines, in which the pressure hull was fully enclosed inside the light hull, but optimised for submerged navigation, unlike earlier designs.</p>
<p>After World War II, approaches split. The Soviet Union changed its designs, basing them on the latest German developments. All post-WWII heavy Soviet and Russian submarines are built with a <a href="/wiki/Double_hull" title="Double hull">double hull</a> structure. American and most other Western submarines retain a single-hull approach. They still have light hull sections in the bow and stern, which house main ballast tanks and provide a hydrodynamically optimized shape, but the main cylindrical hull section has only a single plating layer.</p>
<p>Despite being no longer needed for different shapes, the double-hull approach still has a number of advantages. The ring stiffeners and longitudinals are located between hulls, and the light hull can also be used to mount certain equipment that does not require constant pressure to operate, while attaching it directly to the pressure hull could cause dangerous local stress. These measures save a lot of space inside the pressure hull, which is much heavier and takes longer to build than the light hull. In case the submarine is damaged, the light hull can take most of the damage, which does not compromise the boat's integrity, as long as the strong hull is intact. A light hull can also be acoustically decoupled from the pressure hull, which significantly reduces noise from internal equipment, improves stealth or allows the use of a simpler internal layout and equipment mounting.</p>
<p>The major downside of double-hull structure is the significantly greater amount of manual work required to construct it. The Soviet Union had implemented the requisite welding technology earlier and had enough cheap qualified workers available, but the high cost of manual labor in the United States made the less expensive single-hull approach preferable. Another reason for double-hull construction in the Soviet Union was operation under the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a>, where submarines had to break thick ice to launch their missiles, which could damage the hull. However, the double-hull approach is today being considered for future submarines in the United States as a means to improve payload capacity, stealth and operational reach.<sup id="_ref-0" class="reference"><a href="#_note-0" title="">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a name="Pressure_hull" id="Pressure_hull"></a></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pressure hull">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Pressure hull</span></h4>
<p>The pressure hull is generally constructed of thick high-strength steel with a complex structure and high strength reserve, and is separated with watertight <a href="/wiki/Bulkhead" title="Bulkhead">bulkheads</a> into several <a href="/wiki/Compartments" title="Compartments">compartments</a>. There are also examples of more than two hulls in a submarine, like the <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_class_submarine" title="Typhoon class submarine">Typhoon class</a>, which has two main pressure hulls and three smaller ones for control room, torpedoes and steering gear, while the missile launch system is located between the main hulls.</p>
<p>The dive depth cannot be increased easily. Simply making the hull thicker increases the weight and requires reduction of the weight of onboard equipment, ultimately resulting in a <a href="/wiki/Bathyscaphe" title="Bathyscaphe">bathyscaphe</a>. This is affordable for civilian research submersibles, but not military submarines, so their dive depth was always bound by current technology.</p>
<p>WW1 submarines had their hulls built of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_steel" title="Carbon steel">carbon steel</a>, and could not submerge below 100 meters. During World War Two, high-strength <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloyed steel</a> was introduced, allowing for dive depths of up to 200 meters. High-strength alloyed steel is still the main material for submarines today, with 250-400 meters depth limit, which cannot be exceeded on a military submarine without sacrificing other characteristics. To exceed that limit, a few submarines were built with <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> hulls. Titanium is almost as strong as steel, but lighter, and is also not <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetism" title="Ferromagnetism">ferromagnetic</a>, which is important for stealth. Titanium submarines were favored by the Soviet Union, which developed specialized high-strength alloys and built an industry capable of producing titanium at an affordable cost. It has produced several types of titanium submarines. Titanium alloys allow a major increase in depth, but other systems need to be redesigned to cope, so test depth was limited to 1000 meters for <a href="/wiki/Soviet_submarine_K-278" title="Soviet submarine K-278">K-278 Komsomolets</a>, the deepest-diving military submarine. An <a href="/wiki/Alfa_class_submarine" title="Alfa class submarine">Alfa class submarine</a> may have successfully operated at 1300 meters,<sup id="_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#_note-1" title="">[3]</a></sup> though continuous operation at such depths would be an excessive stress for many submarine systems. Despite its benefits, the high cost of titanium construction led to the abandonment of titanium submarine construction as the Cold War ended.</p>
<p>The task of building a pressure hull is very difficult, as it must withstand a force of several million tons. When the hull is perfectly round in cross-section, the pressure is evenly distributed, and causes only hull compression. If the shape is not perfect, the hull is bent, with several points heavily strained. Inevitable minor deviations are resisted by the stiffener rings, but even a one inch (25&#160;mm) deviation from roundness results in over 30 percent decrease of maximal hydrostatic load and consequently dive depth.<sup id="_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#_note-2" title="">[4]</a></sup> The hull must therefore be constructed with very high precision. All hull parts must be welded without defects, and all joints are checked several times using different methods. This contributes to the very high cost of modern submarines. (For example, each <a href="/wiki/Virginia_class_submarine" title="Virginia class submarine">Virginia-class attack submarine</a> costs 2.6 billion dollars, over $200,000 per <a href="/wiki/Long_ton" title="Long ton">ton</a> of displacement).</p>

<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Propulsion">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Propulsion</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The first mechanically driven submarine was the 1863 French <a href="/wiki/Plongeur" title="Plongeur">Plongeur</a>, which used compressed air for propulsion, and anaerobic propulsion was first employed by the Spanish <a href="/wiki/Ictineo" title="Ictineo">Ictineo II</a> in 1864. Ictineo's engine used a chemical mix containing a peroxide compound to generate heat for steam propulsion while also providing <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> for the crew. The system was not employed again until 1940 when the German Navy tested a system employing the same principles, the <a href="/wiki/Hellmuth_Walter" title="Hellmuth Walter">Walter</a> <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a>, on the experimental <a href="/w/index.php?title=V-80&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="V-80">V-80</a> submarine and later on the naval <a href="/w/index.php?title=U-791&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="U-791">U-791</a> submarine.</p>
<p>Until the advent of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_marine_propulsion" title="Nuclear marine propulsion">nuclear marine propulsion</a>, most 20th century submarines used batteries for running underwater and <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> (petrol) or <a href="/wiki/Diesel" title="Diesel">diesel</a> engines on the surface and to recharge the batteries. Early submarines used gasoline, but this quickly gave way to <a href="/wiki/Paraffin" title="Paraffin">paraffin</a>, then diesel, because of reduced flammability. Diesel-electric became the standard means of propulsion. The diesel or gasoline engine and the electric motor, separated by clutches, were initially on the same shaft and drove the propeller. This allowed the engine to drive the electric motor as a generator to recharge the batteries and also propel the submarine if required. The clutch between the motor and the engine would be disengaged when the submarine dove so that the motor could be used to turn the propeller. The motor could have more than one armature on the shaft, and these could be electrically coupled in series for slow speed and in parallel for high speed. (These alternative connections were known as "group down" and "group up", respectively.)</p>
<p>The principle was modified for some submarine designs in the 1930s, particularly those of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/British_U_class_submarine" title="British U class submarine">British U class submarines</a>. The engine was no longer attached to the motor/propeller drive shaft, but drove a separate generator to drive the motors on the surface while recharging the batteries. This <a href="/wiki/Diesel-electric" title="Diesel-electric">diesel-electric</a> propulsion allowed much more flexibility; for example, the submarine could travel slowly while the engines were running at full power to recharge the batteries as quickly as possible, reducing time spent on the surface, or use its <a href="/wiki/Snorkel" title="Snorkel">snorkel</a>. It was then possible to <a href="/wiki/Soundproofing" title="Soundproofing">insulate</a> the noisy diesel engines from the pressure hull, making the submarine quieter.</p>
<p>Other power sources were attempted. Oil-fired steam turbines powered the British <a href="/wiki/British_K_class_submarine" title="British K class submarine">"K" class submarines</a>, built during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">first World War</a> and in the following years, with the intent of giving them the necessary surface speed to keep up with the British battle fleet. The "K" class subs were not very successful, however. (The design was also over-endowed with hatches, which proved troublesome in service.) <a href="/wiki/German_Type_XXI_submarine" title="German Type XXI submarine">German Type XXI submarines</a> attempted the application of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a> to provide long-term, fast air-independent propulsion, but were ultimately built with very large batteries instead.</p>
<p>At the end of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> and Russians experimented with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a>/<a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a> (paraffin) engines which could be used both above and below the surface. The results were not encouraging enough for this technique to be adopted at the time, and although the Russians deployed a class of submarines with this engine type (codenamed <a href="/wiki/Quebec_class_submarine" title="Quebec class submarine">Quebec</a> by NATO), they were considered unsuccessful. Today several navies use <a href="/wiki/Air-independent_propulsion" title="Air-independent propulsion">air-independent propulsion</a>. Notably <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> uses <a href="/wiki/Stirling_engine" title="Stirling engine">Stirling technology</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Gotland_class_submarine" title="Gotland class submarine">Gotland class</a> and <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B6dermanland_class_submarine" title="Södermanland class submarine">Södermanland class</a> series of submarines. The Stirling engine is heated by burning diesel fuel with <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> stored in <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">cryogenic</a> tanks. A newer development in air-independent propulsion is the use of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cells" title="Fuel cells">fuel cells</a>, first applied in series on the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Type_212_submarine" title="Type 212 submarine">Type 212 submarine</a>, with nine 34 kW or two 120-kilowatt cells.</p>
<p>Steam power was resurrected in the 1950s with the advent of the nuclear-powered steam turbine driving a generator. By removing the requirement for atmospheric oxygen, these submarines can remain submerged indefinitely. (Air is recycled and fresh water is <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distilled</a> from seawater.) These vessels always have a small battery and diesel engine/generator installation for emergency use if the reactors have to be shut down.</p>
<p>Nuclear power is now used in all large submarines, but due to the high cost and large size of nuclear reactors, smaller submarines still use diesel-electric propulsion. The ratio of larger to smaller submarines depends on strategic needs; for instance, the US Navy operates only nuclear submarines,<sup id="_ref-Submarine_Warfare_0" class="reference"><a href="#_note-Submarine_Warfare" title="">[5]</a></sup> which is usually explained by the need for overseas operations. Other major operators rely on a mix of nuclear submarines for strategic purposes and diesel-electric submarines for defensive needs. Most fleets have no nuclear submarines at all, due to the limited availability of nuclear power and submarine technology. Commercial submarines usually rely only on batteries, as they are never expected to operate independently of a mother ship.</p>
<p>Toward the end of the 20th century, some submarines, such as the British Vanguard class, began to be fitted with <a href="/wiki/Pump-jet" title="Pump-jet">pump-jet</a> propulsors instead of propellers. Although these are heavier, more expensive, and less efficient than a propeller, they are significantly quieter, giving an important tactical advantage.</p>
<p>A possible propulsion system for submarines is the <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamic_drive" title="Magnetohydrodynamic drive">magnetohydrodynamic drive</a>, or "caterpillar drive", which has no moving parts. It was popularized in the movie version of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hunt_for_Red_October" title="The Hunt for Red October">The Hunt for Red October</a></i>, written by <a href="/wiki/Tom_Clancy" title="Tom Clancy">Tom Clancy</a>, which portrayed it as a virtually silent system. (In the book, a form of propulsor was used rather than an MHD). Although some experimental surface ships have been built with this propulsion system, speeds have not been as high as expected. In addition, the noise created by bubbles, and the higher power settings that a submarine's reactor would mean that it is unlikely to be considered for any military purpose.</p>
<p><a name="Crew" id="Crew"></a></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Crew">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Crew</span></h2>
<p><a name="Overview_2" id="Overview_2"></a></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Overview</span></h3>
<p>With <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear power</a>, submarines can remain submerged for months at a time. Diesel submarines must periodically resurface or <a href="/wiki/Snorkel" title="Snorkel">snorkel</a> to recharge their batteries. Most modern military submarines are able to generate <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> for their crew by <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of water. Atmosphere control equipment includes a CO<sub>2</sub> scrubber, which uses an amine absorbent to remove the gas from air and diffuse it into waste pumped overboard. A machine that uses a catalyst to convert carbon monoxide into carbon dioxide (removed by the CO<sub>2</sub> scrubber) and bonds hydrogen produced from the ship's storage battery with oxygen in the atmosphere to produce water, also found its use. An atmosphere monitoring system samples the air from different areas of the ship for nitrogen, oxygen, hydrogen, R12 and R114 refrigerant, carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide, and others. Poisonous gases are removed, and oxygen is replenished by use of an oxygen bank located in a main ballast tank. Some heavier submarines have two oxygen bleed stations (forward and aft). The oxygen in the air is sometimes kept a few percent less than atmospheric concentration to reduce fire danger.</p>
<p>Fresh water is produced by either an evaporator or a <a href="/wiki/Reverse_Osmosis" title="Reverse Osmosis">reverse osmosis</a> unit. It is used for showers, sinks, cooking and cleaning. Seawater is used to flush toilets, and the resulting "black water" is stored in a sanitary tank until it is blown overboard using pressurised air or pumped overboard by using a special sanitary pump. The method for blowing sanitaries overboard is difficult to operate, and the German <a href="/wiki/Type_VII_submarine" title="Type VII submarine">Type VIIC</a> boat <a href="/w/index.php?title=Unterseeboot_1206&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Unterseeboot 1206">U-1206</a> was lost with casualties because of a mistake with the toilet. Water from showers and sinks is stored separately in "gray water" tanks, which are pumped overboard using the drain pump.</p>
<p>Trash on modern large submarines is usually disposed of using a tube called a Trash Disposal Unit (TDU), where it is compacted into a galvanised steel can. At the bottom of the TDU is a large ball valve. An ice plug is set on top of the ball valve to protect it, the cans on top of the ice plug. The top breech door is shut, and the TDU is flooded and equalised with sea pressure, the ball valve is opened and the cans fall out to the ocean floor assisted by scrap iron weights inside the cans.</p>
<p>A typical nuclear submarine has a crew of over 120; non-nuclear boats typically have less than half as many. The conditions on a submarine can be difficult because crewmembers must work in isolation for long periods of time, without contact with their families. Submarines normally maintain <a href="/wiki/Radio_silence" title="Radio silence">radio silence</a> to avoid detection. Operating a submarine is dangerous, even in peacetime, and many submarines have been lost in accidents.</p>
<p><a name="Women_as_part_of_crew" id="Women_as_part_of_crew"></a></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Women as part of crew">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Women as part of crew</span></h3>
<p>In 1995 the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Norwegian_Navy" title="Royal Norwegian Navy">Royal Norwegian Navy</a> became the first navy in the world to appoint a female submarine captain.<sup id="_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#_note-3" title="">[6]</a></sup> In 1998, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Navy" title="Royal Australian Navy">Royal Australian Navy</a> (RAN) became the second navy to allow women to serve on combat submarines. Canada and Spain followed in permitting women to serve on military submarines with <a href="/wiki/Seamen" title="Seamen">seamen</a>.<sup id="_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#_note-4" title="">[7]</a></sup> The usual reasons for barring women that are given are lack of privacy and "hot bunking" or "hot racking", a common practice on submarines where three sailors share two bunks on a rotating basis to save space. The US Navy, which permits women to serve on almost every other ship in the fleet, only allows three exceptions for women being on board military submarines: (1) Female civilian technicians for a few days at most; (2) Women <a href="/wiki/Midshipman" title="Midshipman">midshipmen</a> on an overnight during summer training for both Navy <a href="/wiki/ROTC" title="ROTC">ROTC</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Academy" title="United States Naval Academy">Naval Academy</a>; (3) Family members for one-day dependent cruises.<sup id="_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#_note-5" title="">[8]</a></sup> The US Navy argues it would cost $300,000 per bunk to permit women to serve on submarines versus $4,000 per bunk to allow women to serve on aircraft carriers. However, this calculation is based on the assumption of semi segregation of the female crew, possibly to the extent of structural redesign of the vessel.<sup id="_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#_note-6" title="">[9]</a></sup> No studies of the feasibility of an all-female crew, which would circumvent the US Navy's objections, are known to have been carried out. There have been talks, however, about forming an all female submarine and calling it the USS Bob Arbour (SSN 8).</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: History of submarines">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">History of submarines</span></h2>
<dl>
<dd>
<div class="noprint"><i>Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_submarines" title="History of submarines">History of submarines</a></i></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<p><a name="Early_history_of_submarines_and_the_first_submersibles" id="Early_history_of_submarines_and_the_first_submersibles"></a></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Early history of submarines and the first submersibles">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Early history of submarines and the first submersibles</span></h3>
<p>The first submersible with reliable information on its construction was built in 1620 by <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Jacobszoon_Drebbel" title="Cornelius Jacobszoon Drebbel">Cornelius Jacobszoon Drebbel</a>, a Dutchman in the service of <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" title="James I of England">James I</a> of England. It was built to the standards of the design outlined by <a href="/wiki/William_Bourne" title="William Bourne">William Bourne</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Inventor" title="Inventor">inventor</a> of the submarine. It was propelled by means of oars. The precise nature of the submarine type is a matter of some controversy; some claim that it was merely a bell towed by a boat. Two improved types were tested in the <a href="/wiki/Thames" title="Thames">Thames</a> between 1620 and 1624.</p>
<p>Though the first submersible vehicles were tools for exploring under water, it did not take long for inventors to recognize their military potential. The strategic advantages of submarines were set out by Bishop <a href="/wiki/John_Wilkins" title="John Wilkins">John Wilkins</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chester" title="Chester">Chester</a>, England, in <i>Mathematicall Magick</i> in 1648.</p>
<ol>
<li><i>Tis private: a man may thus go to any coast in the world invisibly, without discovery or prevented in his journey.</i></li>
<li><i>Tis safe, from the uncertainty of Tides, and the violence of Tempests, which do never move the sea above five or six paces deep. From Pirates and Robbers which do so infest other voyages; from ice and great frost, which do so much endanger the passages towards the Poles.</i></li>
<li><i>It may be of great advantages against a Navy of enemies, who by this may be undermined in the water and blown up.</i></li>
<li><i>It may be of special use for the relief of any place besieged by water, to convey unto them invisible supplies; and so likewise for the surprisal of any place that is accessible by water.</i></li>
<li><i>It may be of unspeakable benefit for submarine experiments.</i></li>
</ol>
<p><a name="The_first_military_submarines" id="The_first_military_submarines"></a></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The first military submarines">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">The first military submarines</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:Bushnell-turtle-big.jpg" class="internal" title="A cross-section sketch of Bushnell's Turtle."><img alt="A cross-section sketch of Bushnell's Turtle." longdesc="/wiki/Image:Bushnell-turtle-big.jpg" class="thumbimage" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2e/Bushnell-turtle-big.jpg/150px-Bushnell-turtle-big.jpg" width="150" height="224" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/wiki/Image:Bushnell-turtle-big.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A cross-section sketch of Bushnell's <a href="/wiki/Turtle_%28submarine%29" title="Turtle (submarine)"><i>Turtle</i></a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first military submarine was <a href="/wiki/Turtle_%28submarine%29" title="Turtle (submarine)"><i>Turtle</i></a> (1775), a hand-powered egg-shaped device designed by the American <a href="/wiki/David_Bushnell" title="David Bushnell">David Bushnell</a>, to accommodate a single man. It was the first verified submarine capable of independent underwater operation and movement, and the first to use <a href="/wiki/Propeller#Ship.2FSubmarine_propellers_.28screws.29" title="Propeller">screws</a> for propulsion. During the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>, <i>Turtle</i> (operated by Sgt. Ezra Lee, Continental Army) tried and failed to sink a British warship, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Eagle" title="HMS Eagle">HMS <i>Eagle</i></a> (flagship of the blockaders) in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a> harbor on <a href="/wiki/September_7" title="September 7">September 7</a>, <a href="/wiki/1776" title="1776">1776</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:FultonNautilus.jpg" class="internal" title="The Nautilus (1800)"><img alt="The Nautilus (1800)" longdesc="/wiki/Image:FultonNautilus.jpg" class="thumbimage" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/FultonNautilus.jpg/200px-FultonNautilus.jpg" width="200" height="144" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/wiki/Image:FultonNautilus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The <i><a href="/wiki/Nautilus_%281800%29" title="Nautilus (1800)">Nautilus</a></i> (1800)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1800, France built a human-powered submarine designed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fulton" title="Robert Fulton">Robert Fulton</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nautilus_%281800%29" title="Nautilus (1800)"><i>Nautilus</i></a>. The French eventually gave up with the experiment in 1804, as did the British when they later considered Fulton's submarine design.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>, in 1814, Silas Halsey lost his life while using a submarine in an unsuccessful attack on a British warship stationed in <a href="/wiki/New_London%2C_Connecticut" title="New London, Connecticut">New London harbor</a>.</p>
<p>In 1851, a Bavarian artillery corporal, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Bauer" title="Wilhelm Bauer">Wilhelm Bauer</a>, took a submarine designed by him called the <i><a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Bauer#The_Brandtaucher_.28incendiary_diver.29_submarine" title="Wilhelm Bauer">Brandtaucher</a></i> (incendiary-diver) to sea in <a href="/wiki/Kiel" title="Kiel">Kiel</a> Harbour. This submarine was built by <a href="/wiki/August_Howaldt" title="August Howaldt">August Howaldt</a> and powered by a <a href="/wiki/Treadwheel" title="Treadwheel">treadwheel</a>. It sank but the crew of 3 managed to escape. The submarine was raised in 1887 and is on display in a museum in Dresden.</p>
<p><a name="Submarines_in_the_American_Civil_War" id="Submarines_in_the_American_Civil_War"></a></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Submarines in the American Civil War">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Submarines in the American Civil War</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:USS_Alligator_0844401.jpg" class="internal" title="The 1862 Alligator, submarine of the US Navy developed in conjunction with the French"><img alt="The 1862 Alligator, submarine of the US Navy developed in conjunction with the French" longdesc="/wiki/Image:USS_Alligator_0844401.jpg" class="thumbimage" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d2/USS_Alligator_0844401.jpg/200px-USS_Alligator_0844401.jpg" width="200" height="202" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/wiki/Image:USS_Alligator_0844401.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The 1862 <i><a href="/wiki/USS_Alligator_%281862%29" title="USS Alligator (1862)">Alligator</a></i>, submarine of the <a href="/wiki/US_Navy" title="US Navy">US Navy</a> developed in conjunction with the French</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, the Union was the first to field a submarine. The French-designed <a href="/wiki/USS_Alligator_%281862%29" title="USS Alligator (1862)"><i>Alligator</i></a> was the first <a href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> sub and the first to feature compressed air (for air supply) and an air filtration system. It was the first submarine to carry a diver lock which allowed a diver to plant electrically detonated mines on enemy ships. Initially hand-powered by oars, it was converted after 6 months to a screw propeller powered by a hand crank. With a crew of 20, it was larger than Confederate submarines. <i>Alligator</i> was 47 feet (14.3 m) long and about 4 feet (1.2 m) in diameter. It was lost in a storm off <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras" title="Cape Hatteras">Cape Hatteras</a> on <a href="/wiki/April_1" title="April 1">April 1</a>, <a href="/wiki/1863" title="1863">1863</a> while uncrewed and under tow to its first combat deployment at Charleston.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederate States of America</a> fielded several human-powered submarines including <a href="/wiki/H._L._Hunley_%28submarine%29" title="H. L. Hunley (submarine)"><i>H. L. Hunley</i></a> (named for one of its financiers, <a href="/wiki/Horace_Lawson_Hunley" title="Horace Lawson Hunley">Horace Lawson Hunley</a>) . The first Confederate submarine was the 30-foot long <i>Pioneer</i> which sank a target <a href="/wiki/Schooner" title="Schooner">schooner</a> using a towed mine during tests on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Pontchartrain" title="Lake Pontchartrain">Lake Pontchartrain</a> but it was not used in combat. It was scuttled after New Orleans was captured and in 1868 was sold for scrap.</p>
<p><i>Hunley</i> was intended for attacking the North's ships, which were blockading the South's seaports. The submarine had a long pole with an explosive charge in the bow, called a <a href="/wiki/Spar_torpedo" title="Spar torpedo">spar torpedo</a>. The sub had to approach an enemy vessel, attach the explosive, move away, and then detonate it. It was extremely hazardous to operate, and had no air supply other than what was contained inside the main compartment. On two occasions, the sub sank; on the first occasion half the crew died and on the second, the entire eight-man crew (including Hunley himself) drowned. On <a href="/wiki/February_18" title="February 18">February 18</a>, <a href="/wiki/1864" title="1864">1864</a> <i>Hunley</i> sank <a href="/wiki/USS_Housatonic" title="USS Housatonic">USS <i>Housatonic</i></a> off the Charleston Harbor, the first time a submarine successfully sank another ship, though it sank in the same engagement shortly after signaling its success. Another Confederate submarine was lost on its maiden voyage in Lake Pontchartrain; it was found washed ashore in the 1870s and is now on display at the <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_State_Museum" title="Louisiana State Museum">Louisiana State Museum</a>. Submarines did not have a major impact on the outcome of the war, but did portend their coming importance to naval warfare and increased interest in their use in naval warfare.</p>
<p><a name="Latin_America" id="Latin_America"></a></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Latin America">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Latin America</span></h3>
<p>The first submarine in Latin America was the "<a href="/wiki/Flach_%28submarine%29" title="Flach (submarine)">Flach</a>", commissioned in 1865 by the Chilean government during the war between <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> against <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> (1864-1866). It was built by the German engineer Karl Flach. The submarine sank during tests in <a href="/wiki/Valparaiso" title="Valparaiso">Valparaiso</a> bay on <a href="/wiki/May_3" title="May 3">May 3</a>, <a href="/wiki/1866" title="1866">1866</a>, with the entire eleven-man crew.</p>
<p><a name="Mechanically-powered_submarines_.28late_19th_century.29" id="Mechanically-powered_submarines_.28late_19th_century.29"></a></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Mechanically-powered submarines (late 19th century)">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Mechanically-powered submarines (late 19th century)</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:Plongeur.jpg" class="internal" title="Plongeur, the first submarine to rely on mechanical power for propulsion."><img alt="Plongeur, the first submarine to rely on mechanical power for propulsion." longdesc="/wiki/Image:Plongeur.jpg" class="thumbimage" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Plongeur.jpg/200px-Plongeur.jpg" width="200" height="87" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/wiki/Image:Plongeur.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Plongeur" title="Plongeur">Plongeur</a></i>, the first submarine to rely on mechanical power for propulsion.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first submarine not relying on human power for propulsion was the French <i><a href="/wiki/Plongeur" title="Plongeur">Plongeur</a></i>, launched in 1863, and using compressed air at 180 <a href="/wiki/Pound-force_per_square_inch" title="Pound-force per square inch">psi</a>.<sup id="_ref-globalsecurity_0" class="reference"><a href="#_note-globalsecurity" title="">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first combustion-powered submarine was <a href="/wiki/Ictineo" title="Ictineo"><i>Ictineo II</i></a>, designed in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> by <a href="/wiki/Narcis_Monturiol_i_Estarriol" title="Narcis Monturiol i Estarriol">Narcís Monturiol</a>. Originally launched in 1864 for human-power, propelled by 16 men,<sup id="_ref-globalsecurity_1" class="reference"><a href="#_note-globalsecurity" title="">[10]</a></sup> it was converted to peroxide propulsion and steam in 1867. The 14 meter (46 ft)-long craft was designed for a crew of two, dive 30 metres (96 ft), and demonstrated dives of two hours. On the surface it ran on a steam engine, but underwater such an engine would quickly consume the submarine's oxygen, so Monturiol invented an <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic" title="Anaerobic">anaerobic</a> engine. The beauty of this method was, while the engine drove the screw it also released oxygen which was used in the hull for the crew and also fed an auxiliary steam engine.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:Ictineo_II.jpg" class="internal" title="A replica of Monturiol's wooden Ictineo II stands near Barcelona harbor."><img alt="A replica of Monturiol's wooden Ictineo II stands near Barcelona harbor." longdesc="/wiki/Image:Ictineo_II.jpg" class="thumbimage" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/33/Ictineo_II.jpg/200px-Ictineo_II.jpg" width="200" height="150" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/wiki/Image:Ictineo_II.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A replica of <a href="/wiki/Narcis_Monturiol_i_Estarriol" title="Narcis Monturiol i Estarriol">Monturiol</a>'s wooden <i>Ictineo II</i> stands near <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a> harbor.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1870, the French writer, <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>, published the <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> classic <i><a href="/wiki/Twenty_Thousand_Leagues_Under_the_Sea" title="Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea">20,000 Leagues under the Sea</a></i>, which concerns the adventures of a maverick inventor in <a href="/wiki/Nautilus_%28Verne%29" title="Nautilus (Verne)"><i>Nautilus</i></a>, a submarine more advanced than any at the time. The story inspired inventors to build more advanced submarines.</p>
<p>In 1879, the Peruvian government, during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Pacific" title="War of the Pacific">War of the Pacific</a> commissioned and built a submarine, the fully operational <a href="/wiki/Toro_Submarino" title="Toro Submarino">Toro Submarino</a>. It never saw military action before being scuttled after the defeat of that country in the war to prevent its capture by the enemy. The first submarine built in series, however, was human-powered. It was the submarine of the Polish inventor <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Drzewiecki" title="Stefan Drzewiecki">Stefan Drzewiecki</a> — 50 units were built in 1881 for Russian government. In 1884 the same inventor built an electric-powered submarine.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:Ottoman_submarine_Abdulhamid_1886.jpg" class="internal" title="Nordenfelt-designed Ottoman submarine Abdülhamid (1886) was the first submarine in the world to fire a torpedo while submerged. <div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/wiki/Image:Ottoman_submarine_Abdulhamid_1886.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Thorsten_Nordenfelt" title="Thorsten Nordenfelt">Nordenfelt</a>-designed <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> submarine <i>Abdülhamid</i> (1886) was the first submarine in the world to fire a torpedo while submerged.<sup id="_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#_note-7" title="">[11]</a></sup> It and its sister ship, <i>Abdülmecid</i> (1887), were built in pieces by Des Vignes (Chertsey) and <a href="/wiki/Vickers" title="Vickers">Vickers</a> (Sheffield) in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, and were assembled at the Taşkızak Naval Shipyard in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Discussions between the English reverend, <a href="/wiki/George_Garrett" title="George Garrett">George Garrett</a>, and the industrially and commercially adept Swede, <a href="/wiki/Thorsten_Nordenfelt" title="Thorsten Nordenfelt">Thorsten Nordenfelt</a>, led to a series of steam powered submarines. The first was the <i>Nordenfelt I</i>, a 56 tonne, 19.5 metre (64 ft)-long vessel similar to Garret's ill-fated <i>Resurgam</i> (1879), with a range of 240 kilometres (150mi, 130 nm), armed with a single <a href="/wiki/Torpedo" title="Torpedo">torpedo</a>, in 1885. Like <i>Resurgam</i>, <i>Nordenfelt I</i> operated on the surface by steam, then shut down its engine to dive. Underwater the submarine released pressure generated when the engine was running on the surface to provide propulsion for some distance underwater. Greece, fearful of the return of the Ottomans, purchased it. Nordenfelt then built <i>Nordenfelt II</i> (<i>Abdülhamid</i>) in 1886 and <i>Nordenfelt III</i> (<i>Abdülmecid</i>) in 1887, a pair of 30 metre (100 ft)-long submarines with twin <a href="/wiki/Torpedo_tube" title="Torpedo tube">torpedo tubes</a>, for the Ottoman navy. <i>Abdülhamid</i> became the first submarine in history to fire a torpedo submerged.<sup id="_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#_note-8" title="">[12]</a></sup> Nordenfelt's efforts culminated in 1887 with <i>Nordenfelt IV</i> which had twin motors and twin torpedoes. It was sold to the Russians, but proved unstable, ran aground and was scrapped.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:Peral_Submarine_Cartagena%2CES_2007.jpg" class="internal" title="Hull of Peral submarine at Cartagena, Spain"><img alt="Hull of Peral submarine at Cartagena, Spain" longdesc="/wiki/Image:Peral_Submarine_Cartagena%2CES_2007.jpg" class="thumbimage" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Peral_Submarine_Cartagena%2CES_2007.jpg/250px-Peral_Submarine_Cartagena%2CES_2007.jpg" width="250" height="151" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/wiki/Image:Peral_Submarine_Cartagena%2CES_2007.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>On the 8th September, 1888, an electrically powered vessel built by the Spanish engineer and sailor, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Peral" title="Isaac Peral">Isaac Peral</a>, for the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Navy" title="Spanish Navy">Spanish Navy</a> was launched. It had two torpedoes, new air systems, and a hull shape, propeller, and cruciform external controls anticipating much later designs. Its underwater speed was ten knots (19 km/h). In June 1890 Peral's submarine launched a torpedo while submerged. After many successful dives the project was scrapped because of the difficulties of recharging at sea and the short range of battery powered vessels.</p>
<p>Shortly after the French <i><a href="/wiki/Gymnote" title="Gymnote">Gymnote</a></i>, was launched 24 September 1888, another fully functional military submarine. The electrically-powered <i>Gymnote</i> completed 2,000 dives successfully.</p>
<p>Many more designs were built at this time by various inventors, but submarines were not to become effective weapons until the 20th century.</p>
<p><a name="Late_19th_century_to_World_War_I" id="Late_19th_century_to_World_War_I"></a></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Late 19th century to World War I">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Late 19th century to World War I</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:USS_Plunger_%28SS-2%29.jpg" class="internal" title="USS Plunger, launched in 1902"><img alt="USS Plunger, launched in 1902" longdesc="/wiki/Image:USS_Plunger_%28SS-2%29.jpg" class="thumbimage" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/a9/USS_Plunger_%28SS-2%29.jpg" width="200" height="146" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/wiki/Image:USS_Plunger_%28SS-2%29.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
USS <i>Plunger</i>, launched in 1902</div>
</div>

<p>The turn of century era marked a pivotal time in the development of submarines, with a number of important technologies making their debut, as well as the widespread adoption and fielding of submarines by a number of nations. <a href="/wiki/Diesel" title="Diesel">Diesel</a> electric propulsion would become the dominant power system and things such as the periscope would become standardized. Large numbers of experiments were done by countries on effective tactics and weapons for submarines, all of which would culminate in them making a large impact on coming World War I.</p>
<p>In 1895, the <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Irish</a> inventor <a href="/wiki/John_Philip_Holland" title="John Philip Holland">John Philip Holland</a> designed submarines that, for the first time, made use of internal combustion engine power on the surface and electric battery power for submerged operations. On 11 April 1900 the United States Navy purchased the Holland VI and renamed it the USS Holland/SS-1, America's first commissioned submarine. In 1902, Holland received <a href="http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?patentnumber=708553" class="external text" title="http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?patentnumber=708553" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 708,553</a><span class="PDFlink noprint"><a href="http://www.pat2pdf.org/pat2pdf/foo.pl?number=708553" class="external text" title="http://www.pat2pdf.org/pat2pdf/foo.pl?number=708553" rel="nofollow">&#160;</a></span>. Some of his vessels were purchased by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russian_Navy" title="Imperial Russian Navy">Imperial Russian Navy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, and commissioned into their navies around 1900 (1905 for Japan, too late to serve in the war).</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:NarvalSubmarine.jpg" class="internal" title="The 1900 French submarine Narval"><img alt="The 1900 French submarine Narval" longdesc="/wiki/Image:NarvalSubmarine.jpg" class="thumbimage" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/NarvalSubmarine.jpg/250px-NarvalSubmarine.jpg" width="250" height="137" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/wiki/Image:NarvalSubmarine.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The 1900 French submarine <i>Narval</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Commissioned in June 1900, the French steam and electric submarine <i>Narval</i> introduced the classic double-hull design, with a pressure hull inside the outer light hull. These 200-ton ships had a range of over 100 miles on the surface, and over 10 miles underwater. The French submarine <i>Aigrette</i> in 1904 further improved the concept by using a diesel rather than a gasoline engine for surface power. Large numbers of these submarines were built, with seventy-six completed before 1914.</p>
<p><a name="Submarines_during_World_War_I" id="Submarines_during_World_War_I"></a></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Submarines during World War I">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Submarines during World War I</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:U9Submarine.jpg" class="internal" title="German submarine U-9. It sank three British cruisers in a few minutes in September 1914."><img alt="German submarine U-9. <div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/wiki/Image:U9Submarine.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
German submarine <a href="/wiki/Unterseeboot_9_%281910%29" title="Unterseeboot 9 (1910)">U-9</a>. It sank three British <a href="/wiki/Cruiser" title="Cruiser">cruisers</a> in a few minutes in September 1914.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Military submarines first made a significant impact in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. Forces such as the <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">U-boats</a> of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> saw action in the <a href="/wiki/First_Battle_of_the_Atlantic" title="First Battle of the Atlantic">First Battle of the Atlantic</a>, and were responsible for the sinking of <a href="/wiki/RMS_Lusitania" title="RMS Lusitania"><i>Lusitania</i></a>, which was sunk as a result of <a href="/wiki/Unrestricted_submarine_warfare" title="Unrestricted submarine warfare">unrestricted submarine warfare</a> and among the reasons of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> entry in the war.</p>
<p>The U-boats' ability to function as practical war machines relied on new tactics, their numbers, and submarine technologies such as combination diesel/<a href="/wiki/Electric" title="Electric">electric</a> power system developed in the preceding years. More submersibles than true submarines, U-boats operated primarily on the surface using regular engines, submerging occasionally to attack under battery power. They were roughly triangular in cross-section, with a distinct <a href="/wiki/Keel" title="Keel">keel</a>, to control rolling while surfaced, and a distinct bow.</p>
<p>In 1916, a <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbian</a> pilot named Konjovic of the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian" title="Austro-Hungarian">Austro-Hungarian</a> air service, bombed and sank <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> submarine <i>Foucault</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic" title="Adriatic">Adriatic</a>, thus becoming the first man to sink a submarine from the air. Spotting survivors in the water, he landed his <a href="/wiki/Flying_boat" title="Flying boat">flying boat</a> and rescued them, an act for which the French government awarded him special recognition in 1968.</p>
<p><a name="Interwar_developments" id="Interwar_developments"></a></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Interwar developments">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Interwar developments</span></h3>
<p>Various new submarine designs were developed during the interwar years. Among the most notorious ones were <a href="/wiki/Submarine_aircraft_carrier" title="Submarine aircraft carrier">Submarine aircraft carriers</a>, equipped with waterproof hangar and steam catapult and which could launch and recover one or more small seaplanes. The submarine and its plane could then act as a reconnaissance unit ahead of the fleet, an essential role at a time when <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> still did not exist. The first example was the British <a href="/wiki/HMS_M2" title="HMS M2">HMS M2</a>, followed by the French <a href="/wiki/French_submarine_Surcouf" title="French submarine Surcouf"><i>Surcouf</i></a>, and numerous aircraft-carrying submarines in the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy" title="Imperial Japanese Navy">Imperial Japanese Navy</a>. The 1929 <i>Surcouf</i> was also designed as an "underwater cruiser", intended to seek and engage in surface combat.</p>
<p><a name="Submarines_during_World_War_II" id="Submarines_during_World_War_II"></a></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Submarines during World War II">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Submarines during World War II</span></h3>
<p><a name="Germany" id="Germany"></a></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Germany</span></h4>
<dl>
<dd>
<div class="noprint"><i>Main article: <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">U-boat</a></i></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> had the largest submarine fleet during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Due to the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles" title="Treaty of Versailles">Treaty of Versailles</a> limiting the surface navy, the rebuilding of the German surface forces had only begun in earnest a year before the outbreak of World War II. Having no hope of defeating the vastly superior <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> decisively in a surface battle, the German High Command immediately stopped all construction on capital surface ships save the nearly completed <a href="/wiki/Bismarck_class_battleship" title="Bismarck class battleship"><i>Bismarck</i> class battleships</a> and two cruisers and switched the resources to submarines, which could be built more quickly. Though it took most of 1940 to expand the production facilities and get the mass production started, more than a thousand submarines were built by the end of the war.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:U-47s.jpg" class="internal" title="U-47 returns to port after sinking HMS Royal Oak in October 1939. The battlecruiser Scharnhorst is seen in the background."><img alt="U-47 returns to port after sinking HMS Royal Oak in October 1939. The battlecruiser Scharnhorst is seen in the background." longdesc="/wiki/Image:U-47s.jpg" class="thumbimage" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7a/U-47s.jpg/180px-U-47s.jpg" width="180" height="102" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/wiki/Image:U-47s.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/U-47" title="U-47">U-47</a> returns to port after sinking <a href="/wiki/HMS_Royal_Oak_%2808%29" title="HMS Royal Oak (08)">HMS <i>Royal Oak</i></a> in October 1939. The battlecruiser <i><a href="/wiki/German_battlecruiser_Scharnhorst" title="German battlecruiser Scharnhorst">Scharnhorst</a></i> is seen in the background<i>.</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Germany put submarines to devastating effect in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_the_Atlantic" title="Second Battle of the Atlantic">Second Battle of the Atlantic</a> in World War II, attempting but ultimately failing to cut off Britain's supply routes by sinking more ships than Britain could replace. The supply lines were vital to Britain for food and industry, as well as armaments from the USA. Although the U-boats had been updated in the intervening years, the major innovation was improved communications, encrypted using the famous <a href="/wiki/Enigma_machine" title="Enigma machine">Enigma cipher machine</a>. This allowed for mass-attack <a href="/wiki/Military_tactics" title="Military tactics">tactics</a> or "<a href="/wiki/Wolf_pack" title="Wolf pack">wolf packs</a>", (Wolfsrudel), but was also ultimately the U-boats' downfall.</p>
<p>After putting to sea, the U-boats operated mostly on their own trying to find convoys in areas assigned to them by the High Command. If a convoy was found, the submarine did not attack immediately, but shadowed the convoy to allow other submarines in the area to find the convoy. These were then grouped into a larger striking force and attacked the convoy simultaneously, preferably at night while surfaced.</p>
<p>From September 1939 to the beginning of 1943, the <i>Ubootwaffe</i> ("U-boat weapon") scored unprecedented success with these tactics, but were too few to have any decisive success. By the spring of 1943, German U-boat construction was at full capacity, but this was more than nullified by increased numbers of convoy escorts, aircraft, as well as technical advances like <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a>. <a href="/wiki/Huff-Duff" title="Huff-Duff">Huff-Duff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ultra" title="Ultra">Ultra</a> allowed the Allies to route convoys around wolf packs when they detected them from their radio transmissions. The results were devastating: from March to July of that year, over 130 U-boats were lost, 41 in May alone. Concurrent Allied losses dropped dramatically, from 750,000 tons in March, to only 188,000 in July. Although the <a href="/wiki/Second_battle_of_the_Atlantic" title="Second battle of the Atlantic">Second battle of the Atlantic</a> would continue to the last day of the war, the U-boat arm was unable to stem the tide of men and material, paving the way for <a href="/wiki/Operation_Torch" title="Operation Torch">Operation Torch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Husky" title="Operation Husky">Operation Husky</a>, and ultimately, <a href="/wiki/D-Day" title="D-Day">D-Day</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> wrote that the U-boat "peril" was the only thing that ever gave him cause to doubt the Allies' eventual victory.</p>

<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Japan</span></h4>
<dl>
<dd>
<div class="noprint"><i>Main article: <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy_submarines" title="Imperial Japanese Navy submarines">Imperial Japanese Navy submarines</a></i></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:I-400.jpg" class="internal" title="The Imperial Japanese Navy's I-400 class submarine, the largest submarine type of WWII."><img alt="The Imperial Japanese Navy's I-400 class submarine, the largest submarine type of WWII." longdesc="/wiki/Image:I-400.jpg" class="thumbimage" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/dd/I-400.jpg/250px-I-400.jpg" width="250" height="158" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/wiki/Image:I-400.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy" title="Imperial Japanese Navy">Imperial Japanese Navy</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/I-400_class_submarine" title="I-400 class submarine">I-400 class submarine</a></i>, the largest submarine type of WWII.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Japan started their submarine service with five Holland Type VII Class submarines originally built at the Fore River Ship and Engine Company located in Quincy , Ma. The country purchased five submarines from the Electric Boat Company now being run by "outersider" who included Isaac Leopold Rice and other men "of means". These five submarines were developed under th watchful sight of Arthur l. Busch</p>
<p>Japan had the most varied fleet of submarines of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, including manned torpedoes (<a href="/wiki/Kaiten_manned_torpedo" title="Kaiten manned torpedo">Kaiten manned torpedo</a>), midget submarines (<a href="/wiki/Ko-hyoteki_class_submarine" title="Ko-hyoteki class submarine">Ko-hyoteki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kairyu_class_submarine" title="Kairyu class submarine">Kairyu</a>), medium-range submarines, purpose-built supply submarines (many for use by the Army), long-range fleet submarines (many of which carried an aircraft), submarines with the highest submerged speeds of the conflict (<a href="/wiki/I-200_class_submarine" title="I-200 class submarine">Sen taka <i>I-200</i> class submarine</a>), and submarines that could carry multiple bombers (WWII's largest submarine, the <a href="/wiki/I-400_class_submarine" title="I-400 class submarine">Sen toku <i>I-400</i> class submarine</a>). These submarines were also equipped with the most advanced torpedo of the conflict, the oxygen-propelled <a href="/wiki/Long_Lance" title="Long Lance">Type 95</a>.</p>
<p>Nevertheless, despite their technical prowess, Japanese submarines were relatively unsuccessful. They were often used in offensive roles against warships, which were fast, maneuverable and well-defended compared to merchant ships. In 1942, Japanese submarines sank two aircraft carriers among other warships, but were not able to sustain these results afterwards. By the end of the war, submarines were instead often used to transport supplies to island garrisons.</p>
<p><a name="United_States" id="United_States"></a></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">United States</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/Image:USS_Grayback_%28SS_208%29.jpg" class="internal" title="USS Grayback"><img alt="USS Grayback" longdesc="/wiki/Image:USS_Grayback_%28SS_208%29.jpg" class="thumbimage" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/17/USS_Grayback_%28SS_208%29.jpg/180px-USS_Grayback_%28SS_208%29.jpg" width="180" height="76" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/wiki/Image:USS_Grayback_%28SS_208%29.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/USS_Grayback_%28SS-208%29" title="USS Grayback (SS-208)">USS <i>Grayback</i></a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The United States used its submarine force to attack <a href="/wiki/Merchant_ship" title="Merchant ship">merchant shipping</a> (commerce raiding or <i>guerre de course</i>), its submarines destroying more <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> shipping than all other weapons combined.</p>
<p>Where Japan had the finest submarine torpedoes of the war, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> had perhaps the worst: the Mark 14 steam torpedo, with a Mk 6 <a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_influence&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Magnetic influence">magnetic influence</a> exploder and a Mk 5 contact exploder, neither of which was reliable. The depth control mechanism of the Mark 14 was corrected in August 1942, but field trials for the exploders were ordered only in mid-1943, when tests in Hawaii and Australia confirmed the flaws. One attempt to correct the problems resulted in a wakeless, electric torpedo being placed in service, but the losses of <a href="/wiki/USS_Tang_%28SS-306%29" title="USS Tang (SS-306)">USS <i>Tang</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Tullibee_%28SS-284%29" title="USS Tullibee (SS-284)"><i>Tullibee</i></a> resulted from self-inflicted hits by these torpedoes.</p>
<p>During World War II, 314 submarines served in the United States Navy. 111 boats were in commission on <a href="/wiki/December_7" title="December 7">7 December</a> 1941 and 203 submarines from the <a href="/wiki/Gato_class_submarine" title="Gato class submarine"><i>Gato</i>-</a> <a href="/wiki/Balao_class_submarine" title="Balao class submarine"><i>Balao</i>-</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tench_class_submarine" title="Tench class submarine"><i>Tench</i>-classes</a> were commissioned during the war. 52 boats with 3,506 men were lost during hostilities. US submarines sank 1,392 enemy vessels, a total tonnage of 5.3 million tons, including 8 aircraft carriers and over 200 warships.</p>
<p><a name="United_Kingdom" id="United_Kingdom"></a></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">United Kingdom</span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy_Submarine_Service" title="Royal Navy Submarine Service">Royal Navy Submarine Service</a> chiefly operated in inshore waters and tended to only surface by night.<sup class="noprint Template-Fact"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources since April 2007" style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i>citation needed</i>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Its major operating areas were around <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, the Mediterranean (against the Axis supply routes to <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>) and in the Far East. RN submarines operating out of <a href="/wiki/Trincomalee" title="Trincomalee">Trincomalee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> were a constant threat to Japanese shipping passing through the <a href="/wiki/Malacca_Straits" title="Malacca Straits">Malacca Straits</a>.<sup class="noprint Template-Fact"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources since April 2007" style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i>citation needed</i>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the war British submarines sank 2 million tons of enemy shipping and 57 major warships; the latter including 35 submarines. Seventy-four British submarines were lost, half probably to <a href="/wiki/Naval_mine" title="Naval mine">naval mines</a>.<sup id="_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#_note-9" title="">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><a name="The_Snorkel" id="The_Snorkel"></a></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: The Snorkel">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">The Snorkel</span></h4>
<p>Diesel submarines need air to run their engines, and so carried very large <a href="/wiki/Battery_%28electricity%29" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a> for submerged travel. These limited the speed and range of the submarines while submerged. The <a href="/wiki/Snorkel#Submarine_snorkel" title="Snorkel">snorkel</a> (a prewar Dutch invention) was used to allow German submarines to run just under the surface, attempting to avoid detection visually and by <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>. The German navy experimented with engines that would use <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a> to allow diesel fuel to be used while submerged, but technical difficulties were great. The Allies experimented with a variety of detection systems, including chemical sensors to "<a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">smell</a>" the exhaust of submarines.</p>
<p><a name="Modern_submarines" id="Modern_submarines"></a></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Modern submarines">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Modern submarines</span></h3>
<p>In the 1950s, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> partially replaced diesel-electric propulsion. Equipment was also developed to extract <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> from sea water. These two innovations gave submarines the ability to remain submerged for weeks or months, and enabled previously impossible voyages such as <a href="/wiki/USS_Nautilus_%28SSN-571%29" title="USS Nautilus (SSN-571)">USS <i>Nautilus</i></a>' crossing of the North pole beneath the Arctic ice cap in 1958 and the <a href="/wiki/USS_Triton_%28SSRN-586%29" title="USS Triton (SSRN-586)">U.S.S. <i>Triton</i></a>'s submerged circumnavigation of the world in 1960. Most of the naval submarines built since that time in the United States and the Soviet Union/Russia have been powered by nuclear reactors. The limiting factors in submerged endurance for these vessels are food supply and crew morale in the space-limited submarine.</p>
<p>While the greater endurance and performance from nuclear reactors mean that nuclear submarines are better for long distance missions or the protection of a carrier battle-force, conventional diesel-electric submarines have continued to be produced by both nuclear and non-nuclear powers, as they can be made stealthier, except when required to run the diesel engine to recharge the ship’s battery. Technological advances in sound dampening, noise isolation and cancellation have substantially eroded this advantage. Though far less capable regarding speed and weapons payload, conventional submarines are also cheaper to build. The introduction of air-independent propulsion boats led to increased sales numbers of such types of submarines.</p>
<p>During the Cold War, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States of America</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> maintained large submarine fleets that engaged in cat-and-mouse games; this tradition today continues, on a much-reduced scale. The Soviet Union suffered the loss of at least four submarines during this period: <a href="/wiki/Soviet_submarine_K-129" title="Soviet submarine K-129">K-129</a> was lost in 1968 (which the <a href="/wiki/CIA" title="CIA">CIA</a> attempted to retrieve from the ocean floor with the <a href="/wiki/Howard_Hughes" title="Howard Hughes">Howard Hughes</a>-designed ship named <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Glomar_Explorer" title="Hughes Glomar Explorer"><i>Glomar Explorer</i></a>), <a href="/wiki/Soviet_submarine_K-8" title="Soviet submarine K-8"><i>K-8</i></a> in 1970, <a href="/wiki/Soviet_submarine_K-219" title="Soviet submarine K-219">K-219</a> in 1986 (subject of the film "Hostile Waters"), and <a href="/wiki/Soviet_submarine_Komsomolets" title="Soviet submarine Komsomolets"><i>Komsomolets</i></a> (the only Mike class submarine) in 1989 (which held a depth record among military submarines—1000&#160;m). Many other Soviet subs, such as <a href="/wiki/Soviet_submarine_K-19" title="Soviet submarine K-19">K-19</a> (the first Soviet nuclear submarine, and the first Soviet sub to reach the North Pole) were badly damaged by fire or radiation leaks. The United States lost two nuclear submarines during this time: <a href="/wiki/USS_Thresher_%28SSN-593%29" title="USS Thresher (SSN-593)">USS <i>Thresher</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Scorpion_%28SSN-589%29" title="USS Scorpion (SSN-589)"><i>Scorpion</i></a>. <i>Thresher</i> was lost due to equipment failure during a test dive while at its operational limit, and the exact cause of the loss of <i>Scorpion</i> is not known, though it is thought by many to have been a faulty torpedo.</p>
<p>The sinking of <a href="/wiki/PNS_Ghazi" title="PNS Ghazi"><i>Ghazi</i></a> in the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pakistani_War_of_1971" title="Indo-Pakistani War of 1971">Indo-Pakistani War of 1971</a> was the first submarine casualty in the <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asian</a> region. The United Kingdom employed nuclear-powered submarines against <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> in 1982 during the two nations' <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a>. The sinking of the cruiser <a href="/wiki/ARA_General_Belgrano" title="ARA General Belgrano"><i>General Belgrano</i></a> by <a href="/wiki/HMS_Conqueror_%28S48%29" title="HMS Conqueror (S48)">HMS <i>Conqueror</i></a> was the first sinking by a nuclear-powered submarine in war.</p>

<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Film</span></h3>
<dl>
<dd><i>Main article: <a href="/wiki/Submarine_film" title="Submarine film">Submarine film</a></i></dd>
</dl>
<p>A genre of submarine movies has developed. Submarines are popular subjects for films due to the danger, drama and claustrophobia of being on a submarine, and the suspense of the cat-and-mouse game of submarine or anti-submarine warfare. One of the first, based on a classic book, was <i><a href="/wiki/Run_Silent%2C_Run_Deep" title="Run Silent, Run Deep">Run Silent, Run Deep</a></i>. More modern movies include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hunt_for_Red_October_%28film%29" title="The Hunt for Red October (film)">The Hunt for Red October</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Das_Boot" title="Das Boot">Das Boot</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/U-571_%28film%29" title="U-571 (film)">U-571</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Crimson_Tide_%28film%29" title="Crimson Tide (film)">Crimson Tide</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Enemy_Below" title="The Enemy Below">The Enemy Below</a></i>. <i><a href="/wiki/K-19:_The_Widowmaker" title="K-19: The Widowmaker">K-19: The Widowmaker</a></i> is about the first of many disasters that befell the Soviet submarine K-19. <i><a href="/wiki/Operation_Petticoat" title="Operation Petticoat">Operation Petticoat</a></i> is a Cary Grant comedy from 1959 about a World War II submarine. Another comedy about a diesel submarine, <i><a href="/wiki/Down_Periscope" title="Down Periscope">Down Periscope</a></i>, stars <a href="/wiki/Kelsey_Grammer" title="Kelsey Grammer">Kelsey Grammer</a>. The <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a> movie <a href="/wiki/The_Spy_Who_Loved_Me" title="The Spy Who Loved Me">The Spy Who Loved Me</a> featured a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> ballistic missile sub being stolen by a shipping tycoon to be used in his plot for world domination.</p>
